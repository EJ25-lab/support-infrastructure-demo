Discovery through launch
A doable path to fall
Discovery begins in June. Each campus builds in sequence. Your system is running its first full diagnostic cycle with the protocol active by the first week of school. Here is what each stage looks like, who is in the room, and what EJC is doing at every point.
1
Discovery June · Weeks 1–2 ~2 hrs per campus

One focused conversation per campus. No assumptions are made about what is working or what needs to change. The goal is to understand each campus's actual schedule, actual staffing, and how diagnostic data currently moves — or doesn't — from assessment to action.

Who is in the room
the CSO — network context, priorities, what success looks like across all campuses

2
Build June–July · Weeks 3–7 Sequential by campus

Each campus gets its own protocol built to its own staffing and schedule reality. Elementary campuses build first. Middle school follows in sequence. High school closes the build. the CSO reviews each protocol before it goes to the campus principal — nothing is delivered to a building without CSO sign-off.

One working session per campus — teachers, fellows, and the principal in the same room. This is not a training day. It is the session where the protocol becomes theirs. Data, ownership, and next steps
Four things worth naming before the engagement opens — how data moves, who owns what at the end, how the engagement starts, and when it runs.
Data flow
How data moves — and what EJC never touches

EJC does not have direct access to any district student information system. All data used to build and run the protocols moves through your designated data coordinator.

diagnostic exports — delivered by your data coordinator as CSV or Excel on the agreed cycle schedule. EJC builds from exports; no platform login required.

Student Transition Records — generated from data your ES teams have already entered. No additional data entry required from campus staff.

No direct system access. No student PII beyond what appears in coordinator-delivered exports. The district retains all data at all times.

Ownership
What the district owns on the day the engagement closes

Every protocol, guide, monitoring sheet, and view built during this engagement is the district's at engagement close. No license. No recurring fee. No dependency on EJC to run what was built.

All tools and documentation are transferred to your designated network lead at the day-60 confirmation.

All access credentials for any platform layer are transferred to the district at go-live. EJC retains no access after the engagement closes.

The system was built to run without us. That is the point.

What the principal brings
Six items · No preparation required beyond pulling what already exists
1
Current master schedule — intervention block
The actual schedule showing when the Core Literacy or equivalent intervention block runs for each grade, who leads it, and what the block looks like on a typical day. A printed or screen-shared copy is fine.
2
diagnostic report — most recent cycle
The class-level or grade-level diagnostic report from the most recent diagnostic cycle. It does not need to be formatted or summarized — the raw report is exactly what is needed to understand how data currently flows from assessment to action.
3
Fellow roster and current assignments
The list of fellows currently assigned to this campus, which students or groups each fellow is working with, and how those assignments were made. If there is a written assignment sheet or spreadsheet, bring it. If assignments are informal, that is important to know too.
4
Walkthrough observation data — last 90 days
Observation records from the past 90 days for fellows working in the intervention block. The focus is on what is being observed and whether observation cycles are connected to what fellows are being asked to do with students.
5
Any active SBIT or SOC documentation
Current Student of Concern forms or active School-Based Intervention Team documentation for students currently receiving Tier 2 or Tier 3 support. This helps establish the boundary between what the tutoring corps handles and what is already in a formal intervention plan.
6
Five to eight students the principal is thinking about
Not a formal list — just the names that come to mind when the principal asks themselves: which students am I watching right now who are not moving the way I expect them to? These students anchor the entire discovery conversation in real cases rather than abstract process.
A note on preparation: The principal does not need to analyze or summarize any of these materials before the session. Bringing them as they exist — even if they are incomplete or informal — is exactly right. The discovery session is designed to work with reality, not with polished documentation.

The discovery questions
Five areas · Plain language · No right answers
The Schedule How the intervention block actually runs 3 questions ▼
When does the intervention block run for each grade, and who has the authority to protect it when something else competes for that time? What most commonly pulls students out of the block during a typical week?
What this surfaces
Whether the block is structurally protected or whether it runs at the discretion of whoever is managing the day. A block that competes with assemblies, pull-outs, and testing is not a block that can carry a routing protocol — the protocol will collapse the first week a conflict arises.
When a fellow is absent, what happens to the students assigned to that fellow? Is there a sub protocol, a coverage plan, or does the session simply not happen?
What this surfaces
The fragility of the current system. If the intervention depends entirely on one fellow being present, the most vulnerable students lose their support the most often — because coverage gaps fall unevenly. This shapes how the protocol handles continuity.
Walk me through what the block looks like on a day it runs well. What time does it start, who is in the room, what are students doing when they arrive, and how does the fellow know what to work on?
What this surfaces
The gap between the intended model and the actual practice. Principals describe the ideal; what comes out in the details reveals the real structure — or the absence of one.
The Assignment How fellows are matched to students 3 questions ▼
Right now, who makes the decision about which fellow works with which students — and what information goes into that decision? Is it based on the diagnostic, on schedule availability, on teacher request, or on something else?
What this surfaces
Whether assignment is systematic or relational. In most tutor programs, assignments are driven by availability and familiarity — not by what the diagnostic says the student needs. That is not a failure of the people involved; it is a missing protocol.
How often do fellow assignments change during the year — and what triggers a change? Does the diagnostic cycle produce new assignments, or do fellows tend to stay with the same students from September through June?
What this surfaces
Whether the assignment system is dynamic or static. A fellow-student relationship built on familiarity has real value — the protocol does not eliminate it. But if assignments never change regardless of what the diagnostic shows, students who are ready to move up or down in intensity are stuck.
Of the five to eight students you brought to this session — look at each one's current fellow assignment. Was that assignment made because of what the diagnostic said, or for a different reason?
What this surfaces
The real assignment logic, grounded in specific students the principal already knows. This question tends to be where principals recognize the gap themselves — because when they look at actual students, the mismatch between diagnostic data and assignment becomes visible.
The Data Screening, identification, and how data moves to support 3 questions ▼
What does it actually take for a student to be identified for Tutoring Corps support at this campus — and is that threshold written down anywhere? Is it a specific diagnostic score, a score plus a teacher flag, a set number of consecutive cycles below benchmark, or something else? Who makes the final identification decision, and how is it documented?
What this surfaces
Whether identification is systematic or discretionary. In most tutor programs, identification is driven by teacher concern rather than a defined screening threshold — which means students who have a strong relationship with their teacher get referred, and students who don’t may not. A shared threshold that applies consistently across the campus is one of the core outputs of the build. This question establishes what that threshold currently is, or whether one needs to be built from scratch.
When the diagnostic cycle closes and results come in, where do those results actually go — and in what form does the Dean of Fellows or a classroom teacher access them? Is it a report inside the diagnostic platform, a pull the data coordinator runs, a spreadsheet someone maintains, or something else? When the Dean of Fellows sits down to assign fellows for the week, what do they look at, and where did that information come from?
What this surfaces
The distance between the data and the people who need to act on it. A diagnostic score that lives in diagnostic but never reaches the fellow before the session is not a usable data point for the protocol. Your team is already running these cycles — this question is about understanding where the output lands and who touches it before it reaches the fellow. The protocol is built around the data pathway that actually exists, not the one that was intended.
EJC does not access student records directly and does not receive files with student names or ID numbers. Who is the campus data coordinator, and what does a de-identified data pull typically look like for them — a routine export, or something that requires advance planning?
What this surfaces — and why it accelerates the build
To build the routing protocol, EJC needs structural information only: how many students per grade are below benchmark, what the score distribution looks like across the current cycle, and how existing intervention groups are structured. That information travels from the data coordinator to EJC with all student identifiers removed before it leaves the district’s system. Your team is already producing this data — this question determines the lead time needed to package it in a way that protects student privacy and gets the build started without slowing anyone down. A coordinator who can run a de-identified export in an afternoon sets a different build timeline than one who needs two weeks. Both are workable. Knowing the answer here is what allows EJC to sequence the work correctly.
EJC on student data
EJC works from de-identified structural data only. Student names and ID numbers never leave the district’s custody. The district data coordinator owns all data movement within the district’s own environment. EJC receives only the aggregate information needed to build the routing logic — score distributions, group structures, identification thresholds — not individual student records.
The Data Handoff What the fellow knows before the session starts 3 questions ▼
Before a fellow sits down with a student on Monday morning, what do they know about that student? Where did that information come from, and when did they receive it?
What this surfaces
Whether fellows are working from current data or from impressions accumulated over time. In most programs, the answer is that fellows know what they have learned from working with the student — which is valuable, but it is not the same as knowing what the diagnostic said last cycle.
After a fellow session, does the classroom teacher learn what happened? Is there a structured way that information travels from the fellow back to the teacher, or does it depend on those two people finding each other?
What this surfaces
Whether there is a closed loop or an open one. A teacher who submitted a concern about a student and never hears back stops submitting concerns. The protocol has to close the loop — documented, not conversational.
When the diagnostic cycle closes and new scores come in, how does that information reach the fellow? Is there a formal process, or does the fellow access it independently if they know to look?
What this surfaces
The data handoff gap — the most common place where a well-resourced tutor program produces general support instead of targeted intervention. The fellow has to know what the data says before the session, not after it.
The Movement Trigger When and how a student moves up or out 3 questions ▼
When a student has been in the intervention block for a full cycle without showing progress, what triggers the next conversation? Who initiates it, when does it happen, and what are the possible outcomes?
What this surfaces
Whether there is a named decision point or an ambiguous one. In most schools, the answer involves a data meeting at some point — but the specific trigger, the specific person who calls the conversation, and the specific window it operates in are rarely named. That gap is where students stay in the same level of support for an entire year.
Of the five to eight students you brought — for any student who has been in the current support level for more than one diagnostic cycle, what is the documented reason they have not moved? Is that reason written down anywhere?
What this surfaces
Whether movement decisions are documented or assumed. A student who has been
